    A Centaur's Glamour

    "Well," said Erin, "the truth is, most centaurs stay out of the public eye by living somewhere remote and limiting their interaction with the outside world. But that's not always possible for all of us all the time. So, when we have to live somewhere that's crowded, we get our hooves shod with magic horseshoes that muffle our steps and make our horse-halves invisible.

    "It's actually a little more complicated than that, since the magic also puts an illusion of human legs and clothes and shoes around our forelegs, and makes it look like we can sit down in public. Mostly, though, we just have to be really careful about moving around in tight spaces, not bumping into things, and not letting anyone else bump into us."

    "And that actually works?" said John, with apparent skepticism.

    "It's worked so far," said Erin with a shrug of her shoulders. "Unless something's happened since the last time I watched the news."

    "Fair enough," said John. His own little salad-bowl was empty now, and Erin had polished off all the rest of it herself.

    "Wait right here," she said, excusing herself from the table and heading for the kitchen. (Enchanted horseshoes or no, John could definitely hear the clip-clop of her hooves on Erin's hard-wood floor.) "I'll go check on dinner's nest course."

    "Next course?" John echoed.

    A minute later, Erin returned from the kitchen with a pan held via oven-mitts. She set a pot-roast with potatoes on the table and said to John, "Dig in."

    "Looks delicious," said John, while Erin took up a knife and fork and carved the meat.

    "At this point," said Erin, "my date would normally ask, 'don't centaurs have to eat hay or something?'"

    "The thought never crossed my mind," said John. "Cross my heart."

    "I can tell that you're curious about it anyway," said Erin. "I don't mind. It's actually nice to meet a guy who seems to be genuinely interested in what it's like to be a centaur."

    "Now that, I am curious about," said John. After trying the roast and spuds and complimenting Erin on her cooking skills (which made her blush), he said, "All right, I'll bite. I know horses can't digest a lot of meat. But centaurs can?"

    Erin nodded. "We have to eat a lot of roughage, but we like our people-food too. Same as you."

    "I guess that makes sense," John reasoned. "Ancient Greek centaurs are always depicted with a bow and arrow, but they wouldn't have been hunters if they couldn't eat any meat."

    "Oh, hunting is still very important to centaur culture," said Erin. "Remind me to tell you all about it sometime! But in the meanwhile, what would you like to do after dinner?"

    "Well I had been thinking of a movie, but . . . can you go to a theater?"

    "Doable, but risky," said Erin. "I'd have to sit in an aisle. I prefer to go during the day, when theaters are mostly empty anyway."

    "All right," said John. He thought for a moment and then suggested, "How about a walk in the park? It should be pretty nice out this time of the evening."

    "Sounds lovely," said Erin. She pushed away from the table again, trotted over to a wine-rack, and came back with two stemmed glasses and a bottle of something red. "Would you like a drink before we go?"
