    At present they were removing all of the old wiring that had been left in the building before it had been abandoned. A lot of that wiring was placed on the floor in the center of the lobby area near where the two were standing. The two could also hear some load banging come from outside as other work teams were starting to assemble the scaffolding needed to put new glass windows in place. Melbourne glanced down at the wiring that had been brought out from the main hallway on the first floor. It was long and often coiled up, but he could see that it was a good idea that the building be rewired. Several of the removed wires looked that they'd been nibbled on in places, likely by rats and mice that moved into the building, either while it was in service or after it was abandoned. In fact, the crews doing the rewiring had even removed a few old "nests" from inside the ceiling.

    "That depends on how quickly these guys can work," Melbourne answered, "and how quickly the crews putting in the new windows can get them put in. The building has seemed to avoid having major structural and wall cracks that would let water in... I'd guess thanks to the foliage that had grown around it, but the windows being broke DID let water in and new wiring can't go in until they're sealed up. From there, plaster and drywall crews will come in to remove the water damaged internal walls and the electricians can then put in the new wiring and so on. The more we stay out of their way the faster they can work and the faster it'll get done."

    "But how long will that take?" Mandibula asked, sounding a bit impatient.

    "I can't really say," Melbourne answered, "a lot of my job, right now, is making sure an adult caiman doesn't decide to grab someone by the river or that a Jaguar or Puma doesn't come out of the undergrowth and grab someone. I've never worked in construction. Much of what I've known on what needs to be done is very basic common sense sort of things that anyone would know."

With a lot of the debris cleaned from walls around the pool and the grounds cleared from around the building, there wasn't that much left for either Mandibula or Melbourne to do. Melbourne ran some standard patrols and set live traps, mostly as a safety measure. Anything that got into them would be set free, and in a way that they would run back into the rainforest and not towards the hotel. And at times, Melbourne noticed a certain amount of protectiveness from him toward her, particularly when a couple of the workers complained that Mandibula wasn't "double dosed." It struck Mandibula as something like what she remembered her mother doing when she was a young hatchling with regard to driving off potential predators and akin to what Melbourne had told her about how human families worked with the parents protecting their children.

    In a way, Mandibula DID like that. It was strange, as she remembered that before her uplifting, the concept of "family" was really not part of what she understood. There was food, predators, territory, and so on, but family really wasn't a concept that was there. She even remembered that her mother chased her off once she grew to enough of a size that she wasn't completely reliant on her mother for safety. Now, though, as an Animalian, Mandibula had found that "family" was now something that was becoming very important. Melbourne was something like a father to her, and she found that she liked that, though she'd never openly tell him. That acceptance of the concept of family also began to raise ideas in the back of Mandibula's mind on ultimately having a mate of her own and raising a family of her own. After all, the two Mountain Lion Animalians had said they were married and had a family back in Animalia and they looked happy. A part of Mandibula wanted that for herself, though she'd need to wait until she fully matured and a male Black Caiman could be uplifted.

    "I'm going to go for a swim," Mandibula said to Melbourne, "stay cool... maybe get a bite to eat."

    Melbourne nodded, "just be careful."

The river was a place to cool off and while there were potential dangers, they were largely dangers to humans who were not adapted for aquatic life and didn't have the strength or natural armor to resist an attack in the water. Mandibula as a Black Caiman Animalian DID have those advantages and she did enjoy often going into the river to fish, as her strong tail could allow her go after even piranha, as she wouldn't be thrashing about to excite them and her scaled armor was strong enough to allow her to go through their schools and take one or two out. Today, though, Mandibula was more interested in cooling down a bit, which the water would allow. She took a deep breath and closed the clear Nictitating membrane over her eyes before diving in.

    Her swim was leisurely through the Amazon's waters, though Mandibula did move toward the river bottom and let her look around at the sights there. There wasn't anything too out of place or unfamiliar to her, but that didn't mean she didn't enjoy looking to it. However, as she got about a hundred yards from the dock to the hotel, Mandibula soon felt another presence in the river and a big one. She could feel it in the pressure sensors along her jaws. Normally she used them to help her hunt fish under water, but they could pick up movement by just about anything in the water. She turned to look toward the source and soon saw what it was. It was another Black Caiman, though clearly bigger than her. It's scent seemed to mark it as male and too much older than she was. That made sense to her, as full adult caiman had claimed the more prized hunting grounds that were father away from the city and the hotel and thus not having to deal with human activity as much, and that younger caiman like herself and this male were forced away from them. The male soon turned and angled toward her.

He gave no audible signal and as Mandibula moved to swim upstream, the male turned and was soon following her, looking more curious then aggressive. The workers with the scaffolding climbed up as Mandibula walked back into the hotel and with the male caiman following. She eventually found Melbourne seated in a chair in the lobby area and approached him.

    "Melbourne..." Mandibula spoke to him to attract his attention.

    Melbourne turned and noticed the male caiman, still following Mandibula and was quick to jump up, which the male caiman noticed and hissed at.

    "Blimey... you've..." Melbourne exclaimed as he jumped back.

He had managed to hang a pair of hammocks in the room and from support beams that were sturdy enough to hold plenty of weight. Cooking was done outside and away from areas that might lead to a fire, but Mandibula also knew that Melbourne did keep food supplies in an igloo there and some basic eating utensils, like plates, bowls, forks, knives and spoons. It was enough for Mandibula to easily gather the material needed, and the male caiman following her made things easier.

She then dropped the meat and the male caiman caught it. Mandibula could only smile as the male chomped on the meat she'd offered and managed to swallow it after lifting his head.     "Humans?" the male caiman asked.

    "The man you growled at outside," Mandibula answered, "His name is Melbourne."

    "Name?" the male caiman wondered.

    "It's how humans identify individuals," Mandibula answered, "as they all look so alike. My name is Mandibula."

    The male blinked and then thought for a moment, "You can call me Cranio."

    "A fine name," Mandibula answered, "Cranio..."

    "You are pretty," Cranio stated.

    "And you are handsome," Mandibula replied, "but let us get to know each other better before going any further. Besides, we'll need to get you some clothes... Melbourne might be able to loan you some."

    Cranio only responded with a growling hiss, "I will not share with a rival."

    "He's more of a father," Mandibula answered, "at times he can be firm... but he's looked out for me has kept people from being fresh with me."

    "A father..." Cranio said slowly.

    Mandibula nodded, "In what humans would see in their families... let me tell you..."
