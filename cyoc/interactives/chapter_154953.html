    While Erin served the sashimi, John thought about what intrigued him the most. Finally, he decided to ask, "So, were you always a mermaid, or did you somehow get turned into one?"

    "Oh, I was definitely born this way," said Erin with a nervous laugh. "And just to be perfectly clear, that was 'born' and not 'hatched'. Caviar was emphatically not involved."

    John chuckled. "Well that's good to hear."

    Erin shrugged. "It's always the first thing every guy asks about. Thought I'd nip it in the bud and save you the trouble."

    John nodded and tasted the sashimi with a bit of soy. "Wow, this is—mm—really good."

    "Glad you like it," said Erin with a toothy smile. "So what else do you want to know? I'm sure you have a million questions."

    "For starters," said John. "Let's see . . . were both of your parents mermaids too? Or, I mean, a mermaid and a merman?"

    Erin giggled. "I know what you meant. And, yes, they were. But my grandpa on mom's side was a human sailor. A fishing-boat captain."

    "Oh? Did he catch your grandma in a neat, or something like that?"

    "Something like that," said Erin coyly.

    After a moment, John asked, "So . . . humans and mermaids can have children together, then?"

    "Mm-hm," said Erin with a nod. She set down her chopsticks and poured herself and John some more tea. "When humans and demi-humans have kids, they can be born as either parent's species. Fifty-fifty chance each time."

    "No kidding," said John. "I wonder how you explain the genetics of that . . ."

    Erin shrugged. "Search me. As far as I know, nobody's even started trying to figure it out yet."

    "Of course not," said John. "The world doesn't know that mermaids exist. How do you—or, uh, your people, how do they manage that?"
