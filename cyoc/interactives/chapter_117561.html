She was a hippie, flower child. Whatever you want to call someone who experienced the 1960s first hand.     "Never heard of them," confessed Harold. "I'm more a classical composition man myself, although Melissa loves some group called Bruce Hornsby and the Range from Eliza's music collection."
    "I can sympathize with her, because sadly both groups eventually broke up," replied Virginia. "But when my grandmother first introduced me to their music, I didn't realize the time gap between then and when they were actually active, so for better or worse, I developed a school girl crush on their lead singer John Sebastian."

    "Harold?" she asked, gently.
    "Hmmm...?" he finally said in return.
    "Are you all right?"
    "I was just thinking."
    "About what?" she wondered.
    "Us," he replied, looking at nothing in particular.
    "Oh?"
    Then Harold Jenkins turned to face her, taking both of the weretigress' paws within his own. "I hope this isn't too soon, but Virginia McCoy, will you marry me?"
    "Harold? Seriously?" she asked, as a tear started to form in the corner of her eye.
    "Yes. Please say yes. Despite how we got to this point, you're the best thing that happened to me since I moved to Moon Lake. I realize now that I still would have eventually fallen in love with you and became a weretiger for you at some point. But fate or whatever decided I wasted too much time mourning and restarted my life. There will always be a place in my heart for Eliza, but there's plenty of room for you too. Please say yes," he pleaded.

    Harold always had two left feet, or paws; but didn't care how badly he danced. Virginia's happiness was contagious and he managed to keep up with her across the bedroom floor.
    It would not be until she finished the song for the second time that Harold got to kiss the future Mrs. Virginia Jenkins again, but a wonderful romantic time of emotional and physical love was in their immediate future; and neither of them would leave the bedroom until Melissa came home for the night.
