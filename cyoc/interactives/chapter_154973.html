    "I'm a marine biologist, naturally," said Erin. "Not fieldwork, I wouldn't have to live on land for that. Instead, I'm into biochemical and genetic research. Working on my second postdoc at the university."

    "Very impressive," said John sincerely.

    "How about you, what do you do?" asked Erin.

    "I'm a writer," said John.

    Erin was intrigued. "Oh? Published anything I might've read?"

    John suddenly became very self-conscious, blushing and coughing. "Oh, um—I doubt it, not unless the kinds of books you like to read are, um—"

    Erin batted her eyelashes and prompted him to continue. "Yes?"

    "—Um, my best sellers are this series of urban fantasy novels."

    Erin's eyes widened. "You're *that* John Robertson!? I *love* your books!"

    John was totally embarrassed now. "I guess I just would have figured, the reality of what you are versus something some guy made up—"

    "Well I don't read fiction for total accuracy," Erin said with a laugh. "What, you think that just because I'm a real-life 'urban fantasy', I'd look down on human imagination, and—and creativity? Please, my tides aren't that shallow."

    "Even still," said John. "Being a biologist is much cooler. I want to start calling you 'Dr. Cooper' now."

    "Please don't," said Erin, folding her arms and momentarily blocking John's view of her glorious chest.

    "Okay."

    Erin watched John pick at his food in companionable silence for a while. Then she asked him, "What?"

    John looked up and repeated, "What?"

    "What's that look on your face?" asked Erin. "You seemed a million miles away for a little while there."

    "Oh," said John. "I guess I was just thinking . . . now that I've had my first little glimpse of what the world is really like, I don't think that I'll be able to write another book until I've seen more of it."

    "What, you mean like going out and meeting more, uh, 'hidden' people like me?" asked Erin.

    "Yeah, that. And also learning more about how magic really works. And what sorts of, uh, mythical-type beings really exist or don't. All that kind of stuff."

    "Oh, I see," said Erin very seriously. "Now that you've met me, you need a tour-guide into the world's seedy magical underbelly, is that it?"

    "I didn't mean—"

    Erin cut him off by laughing. "I've just decided what we're going to do after dinner. When you're done, we're going out to this little club I know that I think you may enjoy."

    "Why's that?"

    "Because it's a safe place for demi's and the humans who know about us to hang out and blow off steam."

    "You're right," said John gratefully. "I would like that."

    "Then it's settled," said Erin, and she finished off the last of her tea and sashimi.
